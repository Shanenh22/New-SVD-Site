How quickly can I be seen for a toothache in North Dallas?

Toothaches are one of the most common reasons patients call us for same-day care. Whether it is a sharp pain when biting, a constant throb keeping you up at night, or sudden sensitivity to hot and cold, we want to see you today, not next week.

At Spring Valley Dental, we hold emergency slots open every single day. When you call, we will triage your symptoms over the phone and get you in as quickly as possible. Most toothache patients are seen within 2–4 hours of calling.

What are the most common causes of a toothache?

Tooth decay that has reached the inner nerve tissue
A cracked or fractured tooth (can be invisible to the eye)
A dental abscess, bacterial infection at the root tip
A lost or damaged filling exposing the sensitive inner tooth
Gum recession exposing sensitive root surfaces
Tooth grinding (bruxism) causing pressure and jaw pain
Sinus pressure or infection radiating to upper teeth
A partially erupted or impacted tooth pressing against adjacent teeth

When is a toothache a dental emergency?

Severe or throbbing pain that does not respond to pain relievers
Swelling in the jaw, cheek, or neck
Fever combined with tooth pain
Bad taste or pus in the mouth near a painful tooth
Difficulty opening your mouth or swallowing
Pain that woke you up from sleep

These symptoms indicate a possible dental abscess or spreading infection, conditions that require same-day treatment. Do not wait.

Toothache FAQs

Any toothache that is severe, throbbing, or accompanied by swelling, fever, or a bad taste in your mouth is a dental emergency. These are signs of infection that can spread quickly. Do not wait, call (972) 852-2222 now for a same-day appointment.

Over-the-counter pain relievers (ibuprofen or acetaminophen) can reduce inflammation and pain temporarily. Avoid aspirin placed directly on the tooth or gum, it can cause tissue burns. A cold compress on the outside of the face can reduce swelling. These are temporary measures, you need to be seen by a dentist to treat the underlying cause.

The most common causes are: tooth decay that has reached the nerve, a cracked or fractured tooth, a dental abscess (bacterial infection at the root), a lost filling or crown exposing the inner tooth, gum disease, or tooth grinding (bruxism) causing jaw and tooth pain. Only a clinical exam and X-ray can determine the exact cause.

Mild sensitivity from exposed dentin sometimes resolves. However, a toothache caused by decay, infection, or nerve damage will not resolve without treatment, it will almost always worsen. The longer you wait, the more extensive (and expensive) the treatment required. A simple filling caught early is far less invasive than a root canal or extraction later.
