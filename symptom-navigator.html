Dental Symptom Navigator: What's Causing My Tooth Pain and How Soon Should I See a Dentist?

This guided tool from Spring Valley Dental Associates in North Dallas asks six short questions — where the discomfort is, what it feels like, how intense it is, whether warning signs are present, how long it has lasted, and how it affects daily life — then gives a clear, dentist-informed assessment and a recommended timeline for care.

What does a constant, throbbing toothache mean?

A constant, throbbing ache that happens on its own, without hot, cold, or pressure to trigger it, often points to deep inflammation of the tooth's nerve (irreversible pulpitis) or an early dental infection. Because the pulp swells inside a rigid tooth, the pressure has nowhere to go, which is why the pain feels relentless. When swelling, fever, or sleep-disrupting pain accompany it, same-day dental care is recommended.

Why do I feel a sharp pain when I drink something cold?

A sharp twinge that fades within a second or two of cold contact usually signals exposed dentin from a small cavity, worn enamel, gum recession, or a tiny crack. The fact that it fades quickly is reassuring — it suggests the nerve is still healthy and the tooth can often be fixed with a simple filling. A dental exam within a few days keeps the repair small.

Is it serious if hot food causes a deep ache that lingers?

Pain from heat that lingers for more than a few seconds after the food is gone is a stronger warning sign than brief cold sensitivity. Dentists use this exact distinction: pain that stops quickly suggests reversible inflammation, while pain that lingers suggests the nerve is inflamed beyond recovery and may need root canal therapy. Book a prompt evaluation within a couple of days.

What causes a zinging pain when I eat something sweet?

A quick zing with sweets usually means sugar is reaching the dentin through worn enamel, a developing cavity, or a leaking filling. Caught early, this is one of the most affordable problems to treat. Left alone, the decay moves toward the nerve and the fix becomes larger. A dental exam within a few days is the right move.

Should I worry about gums that bleed when I brush or floss?

Healthy gums do not bleed. Consistent bleeding while brushing or flossing is the most common early sign of gingivitis — plaque-driven inflammation along the gumline that affects up to 90% of adults. At this stage it is completely reversible with a professional cleaning. Untreated, it can progress to periodontitis, which permanently destroys the bone that holds your teeth in place.

When is a toothache a dental emergency?

Treat tooth pain as urgent when it is constant and throbbing, comes with facial or gum swelling, fever, a bad or salty taste near the tooth, a loose adult tooth, or pain bad enough to wake you from sleep. These point to active infection that can spread to the jaw, sinuses, or neck. Call an emergency dentist the same day at (972) 852-2222.

Does tooth pain that wakes me at night mean infection?

Pain intense enough to wake you, or that worsens when you lie down, strongly suggests the nerve is inflamed or an abscess is forming. Lying flat raises blood pressure in the head and increases pressure inside an inflamed tooth. This pattern rarely resolves on its own and deserves urgent dental evaluation.

How long can I wait if my tooth pain comes and goes?

Intermittent pain over several weeks still warrants an exam within the week. Pain that disappears does not mean the problem healed — cavities and cracks progress silently, and a nerve that goes quiet may have died, letting infection build painlessly until it surfaces as an emergency.
