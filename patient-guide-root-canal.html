What Happens During a Root Canal

An honest, compassionate, step-by-step guide for anyone facing a root canal, what really happens, and why it is not what you fear.

Medically reviewed by Dr. Nicole Houshmand, DDS · Updated June 2026

If your dentist has told you that you need a root canal, you are probably feeling some anxiety. That is completely normal, and almost always based on outdated stories rather than the reality of modern treatment. The truth is that a root canal relieves pain rather than causing it, and most patients tell us afterward that it was far easier than they expected. Here is exactly what happens, step by step, with no sugar-coating.

The fear versus the reality

The reputation of root canals comes from a time before modern anesthetics and techniques. Today, a root canal is typically about as comfortable as getting a routine filling. The pain people associate with root canals is actually the pain of the infected or inflamed tooth before treatment, the procedure is what makes that pain stop. At Spring Valley Dental we take our time, keep you numb and comfortable, and check in with you throughout.

What happens during a root canal, step by step

  • 1. Numbing. We thoroughly numb the tooth and surrounding area with local anesthetic. We do not begin until you are completely comfortable.
  • 2. Protecting the tooth. A small protective barrier is placed to keep the area clean and dry during treatment.
  • 3. Removing the infection. We create a small opening in the top of the tooth and gently remove the inflamed or infected pulp from inside.
  • 4. Cleaning and shaping. The inner canals are carefully cleaned, disinfected, and shaped. This is the step that ends the infection and the pain.
  • 5. Sealing. The cleaned canals are filled and sealed with a biocompatible material to prevent future infection.
  • 6. Restoring the tooth. A filling and usually a crown are placed to restore full strength and function. Your tooth is saved.

Will it hurt? Pain expectations

During the procedure you should feel pressure and movement but not pain, thanks to the local anesthetic. For patients with significant anxiety or a sensitive gag reflex, we also offer sedation options so the appointment is genuinely relaxed. Afterward, it is normal to have mild tenderness for a few days, which is usually well controlled with over-the-counter pain relievers like ibuprofen.

Recovery timeline

Most people return to work or normal activities the same day or the next day. Here is what to generally expect:

  • First 24–48 hours: mild tenderness around the tooth, easily managed with over-the-counter medication.
  • First few days: chew on the other side until any soreness fully settles.
  • Within about a week: most patients feel completely back to normal.

When to call us

Call us at (972) 852-2222 if you experience severe or increasing pain after a couple of days, swelling that gets worse, or if your bite feels uneven. These are easy to address and we would always rather hear from you. And if you are dealing with a painful tooth right now, do not wait, we hold same-day emergency slots open every day we are open.

Remember: a root canal is the solution to tooth pain, not the cause of it. Saving your natural tooth is almost always better than losing it.
